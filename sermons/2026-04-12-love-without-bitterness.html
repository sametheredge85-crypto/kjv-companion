Love Without Bitterness: The Husband's Call to Mirror Christ

Text: Colossians 3:18–19

Supporting Passages: Ephesians 5 · Hebrews 12 · 1 Peter 3

"Husbands, love your wives, and be not bitter against them." (Colossians 3:19)

Introduction — The Root in the Garden

Dear brothers and sisters, imagine a beautiful garden planted by a loving Father. The roses are in full bloom, the vines are heavy with fruit, and the whole place speaks of life and joy. But beneath the surface, hidden from every eye, a single root begins to grow. It is small at first — just a tiny thread of resentment. Day by day it drinks from the soil of hurt, offense, and unforgiveness. Soon that root pushes upward. Leaves wither. Fruit dies. The entire garden is poisoned.

Beloved, that is exactly what the Bible calls "a root of bitterness." It is not loud. It does not announce itself. But once it springs up, it troubles the heart and defiles many. Today we are going to open God's Word and see how this hidden root especially threatens the Christian home. We will hear a clear command given to every husband. We will see the ruin bitterness brings. We will behold the perfect example of the Lord Jesus Christ, who refused bitterness and loved His bride perfectly. And finally, we will discover the gospel remedy that lets us root out this evil and walk in love that mirrors our Savior.

Will you turn with me in your Bibles to Colossians chapter 3, verses 18 and 19? Let us hear the very words of the Holy Ghost.

I. The Divine Command: Love — and Be Not Bitter

Paul writes under the inspiration of God: "Wives, submit yourselves unto your own husbands, as it is fit in the Lord. Husbands, love your wives, and be not bitter against them."

Notice the balance the Lord gives us. He commands love — active, sacrificial, daily love. Then, in the same breath, He says, "Be not bitter." That little phrase is aimed straight at the husband's heart. The word "bitter" means sharp, harsh, resentful — like sour milk that spoils everything it touches. God knows the male heart can harden under pressure. He knows repeated offenses can tempt a man to withdraw affection, speak with sharpness, or nurse silent anger. Therefore the command is twofold: love and refuse bitterness.

This is not optional. This is the will of God. And it is not merely for the wife's sake; it is first and foremost for the glory of Christ, who has called every believing husband to mirror His own love for the church.

II. The Terrible Ruin: How Bitterness Destroys What God Has Joined

Now let us see what happens when that root is allowed to stay. The writer of Hebrews gives us a solemn picture:

"Follow peace with all men, and holiness, without which no man shall see the Lord: Looking diligently lest any man fail of the grace of God; lest any root of bitterness springing up trouble you, and thereby many be defiled." (Hebrews 12:14–15)

Roots are deep. They go down where no one sees. They must be dug out diligently — sometimes with effort that feels grating and painful. But if we leave them, the ruin is real.

First, bitterness destroys oneness. Marriage is meant to picture "Christ and the church" (Ephesians 5:32). It is two becoming one flesh. But when a husband lets bitterness take root, the home becomes two guarded lives living under the same roof. Conversations grow short. Affection cools. The garden of marriage withers.

Second, it defiles the whole household. "Thereby many be defiled." Children watch. They learn that resentment is normal. The atmosphere grows heavy. Even the church feels the chill when a bitter husband stands to pray or preach.

Third, it hinders prayer and testimony. Peter warns husbands to dwell with their wives "according to knowledge, giving honour unto the wife… that your prayers be not hindered" (1 Peter 3:7). Bitterness erects a wall between the man and God. The very throne of grace feels distant.

Think of the children of Israel in the wilderness. They nursed bitterness against God and Moses. Their complaining poisoned an entire generation and kept them from the Promised Land. The same root can keep a Christian home from the sweet presence of the Lord.

Beloved, this is not theoretical. Bitterness ruins marriages — not because God designed it that way, but because we fail to obey the command. Yet even here the gospel shines: the same God who shows us the ruin also shows us the remedy.

III. The Perfect Example: Christ's Love Without Bitterness

Now lift your eyes higher. Look at the Lord Jesus Christ — our heavenly Bridegroom. He was betrayed, denied, forsaken, and nailed to a cross by the very people He came to save. Yet He never became bitter.

On the night of His betrayal, knowing Judas would sell Him for thirty pieces of silver, Jesus still washed the traitor's feet. He still instituted the Lord's Supper. He still taught His disciples. And on the cross He prayed, "Father, forgive them; for they know not what they do" (Luke 23:34).

The Scripture says of Him: "When he was reviled, reviled not again; when he suffered, he threatened not; but committed himself to him that judgeth righteously" (1 Peter 2:23). He loved the church — His bride — unto the end (John 13:1). He gave Himself for her completely (Ephesians 5:25). He sanctifies and cleanses her "with the washing of water by the word" (Ephesians 5:26) without a trace of resentment.

Brothers, this is our pattern. We are called to love our wives "even as Christ also loved the church." Not with a love that pretends sin does not exist, but with a love that refuses the root of bitterness. Christ's finished work on Calvary is the power that makes such love possible. He who refused bitterness now lives in every believing husband by His Spirit. Hallelujah!

IV. The Gospel Remedy: Rooting Out Bitterness by Grace

So how do we root out this deep, stubborn root? Not by trying harder in our own strength, but by looking diligently to the grace of God.

First, remember how much you have been forgiven. "And be ye kind one to another, tenderhearted, forgiving one another, even as God for Christ's sake hath forgiven you" (Ephesians 4:31–32). When we remember the mountain of our own debt that Christ paid in full, the offenses of others shrink in comparison. Forgiveness is not saying the sin did not happen. It is releasing the debt into the hands of the righteous Judge — for Christ's sake.

Second, actively put off bitterness and put on its opposite. The same verse tells us to lay aside "all bitterness, and wrath, and anger" and to choose kindness and tenderheartedness instead. This is daily work. It may feel grating. But it is your responsibility before God.

Third, walk in the Spirit. Galatians 5:22–23 tells us the fruit of the Spirit is "love, joy, peace…" When we are filled with the Spirit, bitterness cannot thrive.

Practical steps for every husband:

  • Begin each day confessing any root of resentment to the Lord and claiming the cleansing blood of Jesus.
  • Bring your marriage before godly brethren for prayer and accountability.
  • Where wisdom requires boundaries, set them in meekness — not in wrath — so that love still rules.
  • Fix your eyes on Christ daily. The more you see His love for His bride, the more you will desire to mirror it.

Conclusion — Call to Christ

Beloved church, the Scripture cannot be broken. The root of bitterness is real, its ruin is terrible, but the remedy is glorious. The Lord Jesus Christ, who loved His bride without bitterness, now calls every husband here to do the same. And He supplies the grace to obey.

If you are a husband struggling with this root today, the Savior who refused bitterness stands ready to root it out of your heart. If you are a wife or a single believer, you can pray and encourage those around you. Together, let us magnify the Lord who loved us and gave Himself for us.

Let us pray.
